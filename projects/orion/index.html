Abstract

Learning robust navigation policies directly from visual observations remains a fundamental challenge in vision-based robotic navigation. In end-to-end imitation learning approaches, the visual encoder and action decoder are jointly optimized using a single action loss, which provides only an indirect supervisory signal to the encoder. This indirect supervision frequently results in the encoder learning ambiguous, action-agnostic representations. The problem is further complicated by substantial variations in scene structure and appearance across diverse environments, as well as the prevalence of visual distractors inherent to real-world navigation settings. Such action-agnostic features cause the navigation policy to produce inconsistent actions at ambiguous decision points, leading to navigation failure.

To overcome these limitations, we propose ORION (Ordinal Neural Collapse for Visual Navigation), a method that explicitly organizes the encoder's representation space according to the ordinal structure of navigation actions. In the context of goal-directed navigation, ego-centric control categories from Far Left to Far Right exhibit a natural ordinal relationship in which neighboring classes share similar visual contexts, while semantically opposing classes differ substantially in appearance. We encourage class representations to be arranged sequentially along a single discriminative axis, while suppressing off-axis variance within each class. The pretrained encoder is then integrated into a diffusion-based navigation framework, and the full pipeline is fine-tuned end-to-end.

Extensive experiments in both simulation and real-world settings show that ORION consistently outperforms end-to-end and neural collapse baselines in navigation success rate and goal progress, with notable gains in visually challenging scenarios such as complex multi-way intersections.

Key Ideas

  • Ordinal structure of navigation actions. Ego-centric control categories from Far Left to Far Right form a natural ordering — neighboring actions share visual context, opposing actions look very different. ORION treats this ordering as a prior.
  • Ordinal neural collapse objective. Class features are pulled sequentially onto a single discriminative axis while off-axis, within-class variance is suppressed — a structured alternative to the indirect, action-agnostic supervision of end-to-end training.
  • Diffusion-based policy integration. The ordinally-structured encoder is plugged into a diffusion navigation framework and fine-tuned end-to-end, keeping the representation prior while adapting to the policy.
  • Robust at ambiguous decision points. The learned axis yields consistent actions in visually challenging scenarios such as complex multi-way intersections, in both simulation and the real world.

Method

ORION pretrains the visual encoder with an ordinal neural collapse objective that arranges the per-class feature means as equally spaced points along one axis, ordered by the navigation action they correspond to. Within-class features collapse toward their class mean, and off-axis variance is penalized, so the geometry of the representation space directly mirrors the geometry of the action space.

The resulting encoder is integrated into a diffusion-based navigation policy and the full system is fine-tuned end-to-end, preserving the ordinal prior while adapting features to the downstream controller.

Two-stage pipeline. Stage 1 (ORION pretraining): the vision encoder is trained with an ordinal neural collapse objective — class means for actions from Far Left to Far Right are arranged sequentially along a single ordinal axis (ℒord + ℒorth) while within-class variance is suppressed (ℒCDNV). Stage 2 (end-to-end training): the pretrained encoder is integrated with a Transformer and a diffusion policy and fine-tuned end-to-end with the action loss.

Results

Across simulation and real-world benchmarks, ORION improves navigation success rate and goal progress over both end-to-end imitation learning (ViNT, NoMaD) and a neural-collapse baseline (NC-ETF). The gains are largest in visually challenging, ambiguous scenes: on the Citysim Multi-Intersection route ORION reaches 74% success versus 48% for NoMaD, and on the Indoor Intersection route it jumps from 17% to 91% — while cutting heading jerk by 71% at intersections, reflecting far more temporally consistent actions.
